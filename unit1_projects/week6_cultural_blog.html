Stereotype Threat

Phase 0 Unit 2: Week 6


Stereotype threat is a theory coined by Claude Steele. He defines it as such: a person's "social identity" has significance when "rooted in concrete situations." A person's social identity is defined as group membership in categories such as age, gender, religion and ethnicity. Steele identifies conrete situations as "identity contingencies"-settings in which a person is treated according to a specific social identity.


Steels also finds that when a person's social identity is attached to a negative stereotype, that person will tend to underperform in a manner consistent with the stereotype. He attributes the underperformance to a person's anxiety that he or she will conform to the negative stereotype. The anxiety manifests itself in various ways, including distraction and increased body temperature, all of which diminish performace level.


I'm glad that Steele's message was not without hope. He stressed that abilities are expandable and that there is no truth to allegations that a particular group lacks a particular capacity. It is important to stress that stereotype threat would continue as the "default setting" until steps are taken to counteract it. One way to combat stereotype threat is to nuture a growth mindset.


Our minds are constantly monitoring and interpreting everything that's happening to us. This is normal and is what it means to be human. Stereotype threat is what happens when that monitoring and interpreting goes bonkers and the result of that is an exaggerated reaction of anxiety, depression, or anger. People with growth mindset use the information they collect to look for areas of learning and constructive action.


Simply, this means changing our inner monologue. Someone with a fixed mindset says this: "This means I'm a loser." "This means I'm a better person than they are." "This means I'm a bad husband." "This means my partner is selfish." Instead, we should focus on questions like these: What can I learn from this? How can I improve? How can I help my partner do this better? This change is needed to counteract stereotype threat.


Dev Bootcamp does a great job integrating feedback for its students. Feedback is given via students and instructors. I believe Dev Bootcamp does this because learning requires soliciting, receiving and integrating feedback. All of which is essential to nuturing a growth mindset. Thus, Dev Bootcamp facilitates the guiding of one's internal monologue from a stereotypical threatening one to a growth-oriented one.
